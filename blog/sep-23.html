week 1, the end of a new beginning

by arlene

my poor sweat glands have been no match for this humid tokyo heat. thankfully it’s tempering down as i write this.

it’s difficult to describe why precisely i love this city so much -- truthfully, it’s the accumulation of all the little details of every day life here that i cherish deeply. no country is perfect, but in many ways my experiences here have felt refreshing for the soul.
if i had to summarize:
i love beautiful things and there are a LOT of them in japan; this whole country is a beautiful thing.
i love the sheer abundance of entertainment, nature, food, relaxation, and shopping experiences, made especially possible by the most gorgeous public transit system. what couldn’t you do here?
i love the attention to detail and how highly engineered and well-designed even the small things are. a very mundane example i recently discovered is the fact that the inside of my duvet cover here has strings on the corners that can be tied to these loops in the corners of my duvet and hold it in place.
see what i mean? the tiny little things.
(if you're interested in hearing more, i'm going to try to keep a list here).
i’m lucky to say it’s my fourth time in japan in the last year and a half, but as you might expect, it feels very different. naturally, to not only be moving here but also attend school here comes with responsibility that was absent in all my previous time here. it’s a little jarring noticing how little i explored and experienced in tokyo in the last week, compared to how much i usually can jam into one day of being a tourist. a reminder, then, that everything is relative. because i've been on vacation here -- when i had maximum flexibility and freedom (and a little bit of time-constrained pressure) -- this week feels boring by comparison. when i didn’t have to go to city hall, learn about a new school system, and run around a bunch of mujis, nitoris, and daisos (home goods stores) to furnish my place.

i might owe myself a little grace as i settle in here. sometimes i push myself in my pursuits to a point of counterproductivity. i’m much more aware of it than i used to be but i still feel this tension between gratitude and comfort. meaning, because i feel so lucky to be here, i feel motivated (and maybe pressured) to make the most of this opportunity. get out there, try not to recede into the comfortable. but realistically, the tourist itinerary is not sustainable anyhow. i do look forward to finding my rhythm here.

i’m a little nervous about consistently updating a blog weekly and publicly, but documenting my life is important to the sentimental part of me. the format is not fully clear to me yet, but i’d like to share moments from the week like:

a memorable meal: i’ve been almost exclusively dining out (much more financially accessible than it is back home), and i walked into a chinese restaurant because i knew i could order some much needed fibre with a vegetable stir fry. after i said 一人でお願いします (“for one please”) she replied 一个人吗？ (“for one?” … but in chinese)
i later asked her
> how did you know i was chinese?
> i could just tell, just like how you could probably tell i’m chinese and not japanese if you saw me on the street.
> haha i don’t know about that, i was raised overseas.
she then asks me about where i’m from, what i’m doing here. she even tells me to talk to her coworker my age who is trying to learn english. this sort of small talk is less common here, i was grateful to have had it there. plus the food was great :)

something that challenged me: one of the hardest parts about learning this new language for me is getting over the HUMILIATION of using it knowing my japanese s*cks, especially as someone who is deliberate about communication and strives to come across exactly the way i want to. i have a bad habit of rushing through my ありがとうございます (“thank you”) because it used to be very difficult for me to say, it sounds more like ありざます. i’ve been trying to correct it and slow it down, and happy to say i’ve noticed that i’ve shaken off a lot of the fear and anxiety around using japanese. as when overcoming the fear of public speaking, you realize that no matter how painfully awkward, anything is survivable. i now embrace the awkwardness and if i need to, i'm not afraid to ask もう一度お願いします(“one more time please”)

something i learned: how to do japanese laundry! specifically, drying stuff outside is quite foreign to me. i learned to set up a laundry line with the laundry pole system built into my balcony.

i think it would be nice to ask my tokyo friends to also write a short guest entry about their week!
if there is anything you'd like me to write about, i would love to hear about it.
